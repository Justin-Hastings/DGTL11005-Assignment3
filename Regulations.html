Regulations Related to Fishing


Like all sports and activities, there are specific rules for fishing and these must be adhered to whilst following all other state laws.

A key example of these rules is not being able to fish in specified areas, usually private marinas and other privately owned wharfs. It is also advisable to not enter private property for fishing unless permission has been given as this is also classified as trespass.

All marine rules must also be followed when using a boat, especially speed and proximity regulations.


Size and Bag Limits

Size and bag limits exist to ensure fish will still be around for future generations by allowing fish to breed and by stopping individuals from clearing out whole schools of fish in one go. These limits are designed to help fishing continue at a high level, so breaking these rules will impact the future of fishing. There are also regulations for crabbing.

If you are caught not complying with these regulations, you will face penalties, including hefty fines and possibly even jail time.

Identifying Fish

Identifying fish can be difficult especially for species that look similar. A fish identification book or app (which can be downloaded from here) is a great tool for resolving this issue. The books are available at most local tackle stores or one can be purchased from here.

Measuring Fish

Measuring fish is an easy way to avoid keeping undersized fish. For measuring fish, a brag mat or measuring board are better than a tape measure. Fish can be categorised as having a forked tail or a flat tail and they are measured differently. To accurately measure a fish, follow these steps and then do step 3 for the required tail type:

  1. Place the fish on the board
  2. Place the head on the 0cm mark


Fish with a forked tail:

  1. Take the fish measurement from the centre of the forked tail


Fish with a square tail:

  1. Take the fish measurement from the outer edge of the tail

Zoning Laws

Zoning rules protect areas with an environmental risk and to control the numbers of some species. Most landbased spots aren’t impacted by marine zonings, however, it’s a good idea to check where you are going before leaving to ensure you are allowed to fish there because not knowing the zones isn't an excuse for breaking the law. Some zones don’t allow fishing at all, like green zones and some just limit fishing, like yellow zones.

To check the zones, you could download the Queensland Zoning app, purchase a paper map from a tackle store or download a map
